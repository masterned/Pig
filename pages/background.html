Background

Origin

Not much seems to be known about when the original game was created nor who created it. The rules of the game were written down for the first time in 1945 by John Scarne, an American magician, in his book Scarne on Dice. Many versions since then (as addressed in the Rules & Variations page) have been released, adding their own twists to the rules.

Its Significance

Though its rules are relatively simple in comparison with other table-top games, Pig has sparked interest in areas even outside of entertainment. For instance, practical, academic, and moral lessons can be learned from the game if studied at the right angle.

In academia, it is often used in middle school to teach the mechanics of probabilities in mathematics and statistics. In upper-level studies of computer sciences, the game is used to teach game theory and AI/ML strategy.

In practical and moral skills, it teaches about the potential loss caused by greed. The incredible about of randomness also serves to level the playing field, allowing anyone of any skill level to compete with nearly the same odds. A game such as this could be leveraged as an effective teaching tool to help young children learn to take turns and to know losing is not the end of the world.
